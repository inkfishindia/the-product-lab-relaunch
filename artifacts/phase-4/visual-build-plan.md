- **What:** Shoot 3 products (1 keychain, 1 card sticker, 1 enamel pin) on the dark posterboard setup to validate that colorful products read well against #1A1A1A-equivalent dark surfaces under phone-camera conditions.
- **Why:** The entire visual system depends on dark backgrounds making products pop. If the dark setup makes products look muddy, dull, or lost, we need to know before shooting 50+ SKUs. This is a go/no-go gate for the photography approach.

- **Failure protocol:** If the dark bg does not work for a product category (e.g., dark-colored keychains disappear), test with a sheet of white paper positioned behind the product to create a rim-light effect. If that also fails, that specific product category shoots on dark gray concrete or dark fabric instead of pure black posterboard.

### 2.2 The Dark Background Test (Do This First)

This is the single most important step in the entire photography plan. The dark-bg system is untested with TPL's actual products under phone-camera conditions. Test before committing.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Clear a table or desk near a wall. Position it so no windows are directly behind the shooting area (backlighting ruins dark-bg shots).

2. Lay one sheet of black posterboard flat on the table. Curve a second sheet up against the wall behind it, creating a seamless dark background (no visible horizon line where table meets wall). Tape the upper edge of the vertical sheet to the wall.

3. Position the desk lamp at a 45-degree angle from the product position, roughly 30-40cm away, pointing down at the product area. The lamp should be above and to the left (or right -- pick one side and stay consistent for the whole shoot).

4. Turn off all other room lights. Close blinds/curtains to reduce ambient light. The desk lamp should be the only significant light source.

5. Place Product 1 (a colorful keychain -- pick one with bright colors) on the black posterboard, roughly centered.

6. Open your phone camera app. Settings:
   - Turn OFF HDR (HDR brightens shadows and kills the dark-bg effect)
   - Turn OFF flash
   - Turn OFF portrait mode
   - Turn OFF beauty/filter modes
   - Set exposure manually if possible: tap the product on screen, then drag the exposure slider DOWN slightly until the background is truly dark but the product is still well-lit
   - If your phone has manual/pro mode: ISO 100-200, shutter speed auto, white balance "tungsten" or "warm" if the lamp is warm-white

7. Shoot 3 angles:
   - Angle A: 45 degrees from above, looking down at the product
   - Angle B: Eye level, straight on
   - Angle C: Slightly elevated, looking down at 20 degrees

8. Review immediately on the phone screen:
   - Is the product the brightest thing in frame? (Yes = good)
   - Can you see product edges clearly against the dark background? (Yes = good)
   - Is there a soft shadow on one side of the product? (Yes = good)
   - Are the product's colors vivid and accurate? (Yes = good)
   - Does the background look truly dark, not muddy gray? (Yes = good)

9. Repeat with Product 2 (a card sticker -- flat, thin product) and Product 3 (an enamel pin -- small, reflective product).

**Troubleshooting:**

| Problem | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Background looks gray, not black | Reduce exposure. Move the lamp closer to the product and further from the background. The key is that light hits the product but not the posterboard behind it. |
| Product looks too dark / colors are dull | Move lamp closer to product. Angle lamp more directly at the product. Use a white sheet of paper on the opposite side of the lamp to bounce light back onto the product's dark side. |
| Harsh/ugly shadow | Move lamp further away from the product. This softens the shadow. Or diffuse the lamp by taping a sheet of white paper over the bulb (not touching -- leave a gap for heat). |
| Product is reflective and has glare spots (pins, metal keychains) | Angle the lamp so it does not reflect directly into the camera. Move the lamp higher. Shoot from a slightly different angle. |
| Dark-colored products disappear against the dark bg | Place a small strip of white paper behind the product (not visible in frame) to create a subtle rim light. Or switch to dark gray fabric instead of pure black posterboard for that specific product. |
| Photos look grainy / noisy | Too little light. Move lamp closer. If your phone has manual ISO, keep it at 100-200. |

**Decision point:** After shooting all 3 test products, review the images on a desktop/laptop screen (not just the phone). If the images look professional enough to put on an ecommerce product page, proceed to 1.3. If they do not, post the test shots in the project channel for Sean/Harley review before shooting the full catalog.

### 2.3 Batch Shooting Process (After Test Passes)

**Setup time:** 30 minutes (one-time per session, already done if continuing from test).

**Per-product time:** 5-8 minutes for 3 standard angles. Add 5-10 minutes per product for lifestyle/context shots.

**Batch size:** Shoot 10-15 products per session. After 15 products, fatigue reduces quality. Take a break.

**Session length:** 2-2.5 hours per batch of 10-15 products.

**Workflow per product:**

1. Place product on posterboard. Adjust position so it is centered and the lamp light falls correctly.
2. Take hero shot (45-degree angle from above). Review on phone. Retake if needed.
3. Take detail shot (close-up of key design element -- text, enamel, print quality).
4. Take back/reverse shot (shows construction quality, clasp, material).
5. Take scale shot (place a Rs.10 coin next to the product for size reference).
6. Optional: take lifestyle shot if this is a hero product (clip keychain to bag, place sticker on card, etc.).
7. Move to next product. Do not stop to edit -- edit in a separate session after shooting.

**Post-processing (batch all edits at once, after shooting):**

1. Transfer all photos to laptop/desktop.
2. Open in Canva or any free photo editor.
3. Crop to square (1:1 ratio). Product should fill 60-70% of the frame.
4. Adjust brightness/contrast: increase contrast slightly to deepen the dark background. Do not oversaturate colors.
5. Export as JPEG at 85% quality. Target file size: 100-200KB per image (fast loading on 4G).
6. Name files per Casey's convention: `[product-slug]-hero.jpg`, `[product-slug]-detail.jpg`, etc.

**Time estimate for full catalog:**

| Catalog Size | Shooting Sessions | Total Shooting Time | Editing Time | Total |
|-------------|-------------------|--------------------:|--------------|------:|
| 30 products | 2 sessions | 5 hours | 2 hours | 7 hours |
| 50 products | 3-4 sessions | 8 hours | 3 hours | 11 hours |
| 100 products | 7 sessions | 15 hours | 5 hours | 20 hours |

### 2.4 Common Mistakes That Make Photos Look Wrong

1. **Shooting with room lights on.** The ambient light washes out the dark background. Turn off everything except the desk lamp.

2. **Using HDR mode.** HDR specifically brightens dark areas -- the exact opposite of what we want. It turns the black background into muddy gray. Turn it off.

3. **Using flash.** Flash creates flat, front-lit images with harsh shadows behind the product. The desk lamp provides directional light that creates depth. No flash, ever.

4. **Shooting at eye level for flat products.** Card stickers and flat items shot at eye level become thin lines. Shoot flat products from 45 degrees above to show the full surface.

5. **Visible posterboard edges.** If the edge of the black posterboard is visible in the frame, it breaks the "infinite dark background" illusion. Adjust crop or camera position to eliminate edges.

6. **Inconsistent light direction.** If you move the lamp between products, every product looks like it was shot in a different environment. Pick a lamp position and leave it for the entire session.

7. **Over-editing colors.** The products are already colorful. Pushing saturation in post makes them look fake. Adjust contrast, not saturation.

8. **Shooting at phone arm's length.** Get the phone close to the product (20-30cm away). Fill the frame. Small products shot from far away look like they are floating in a void, not presented with intention.

- Dan knows dieline construction from his printing background. If needed, a free dieline template generator is at templatemaker.nl (enter dimensions, download PDF dieline, import into Canva as background layer).
- Front panel: #1A1A1A fill, TPL monogram centered in warm white.
- Back panel: Solid accent color fill (#E63B2E or #F2D024 or #E6357A). Product name in Barlow Condensed Bold, sized to fill 80% of panel width. Color of text: warm white on red/magenta, off-black on yellow.
- Side panels: #1A1A1A fill.
- Tuck flap: #1A1A1A fill, "theproductlab.in" in Inter Regular, very small, warm white.
- Inside: unprinted (saves cost), or #1A1A1A if Dan is screen-printing the full sheet.

**Recommendation:** Dan prints in-house. He owns the production facility and has screen printing capability. This gives fastest turnaround, lowest cost, and ability to print small batches of each colorway.

**Assembly:**

1. Print the flat sheet (all panels on one piece of card stock).
2. Score fold lines using a metal ruler and bone folder (or the back of a butter knife against a ruler). Score lines: between front panel and side panel, between side panel and back panel, between back panel and tuck flap.
3. Fold along scored lines. Front panel folds up, tuck flap folds over to close.
4. No glue needed -- the tuck-flap design holds by friction. If boxes do not stay closed, use a small circle sticker (TPL monogram sticker seal, see below) to secure.

**Sticker seal:** Print TPL monogram on signal red (#E63B2E) circular stickers, 25mm diameter. These seal the tuck flap and add a branded finishing touch. Print on sticker paper in-house (Rs.1-2 per seal) or order from a sticker printer at Rs.0.50-1.00 per seal in quantities of 500+.
