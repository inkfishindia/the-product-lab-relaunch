## The Brand in Words

**Record store soul. Candy store body.**

The voice is confident and curated on one hand — we have a point of view, we stocked this shelf deliberately, not everything belongs here. And warm and fun on the other — come in, touch everything, pick up more than one, nothing is precious.

The copy is never:
- Trying too hard to be cool (that's the opposite of confident)
- Ironic for irony's sake (that's a defense mechanism, not a voice)
- Cute or sweet (wrong territory entirely)
- Corporate-casual ("hey there!" startup voice — not us)
- Aspirational lifestyle (we're not selling a better version of yourself)

The copy is always:
- Direct. One idea per sentence.
- Specific. "That's literally you" lands harder than "express yourself."
- Funny when something is actually funny. Not performing funny.
- Short enough to forward on WhatsApp without editing.

## The Purchase Mechanic — Find. Collect. Gift.

Every copy context serves one of three moments. Know which moment you're writing for.

### Find
The sticker that's exactly you. First purchase. The "that's literally me" recognition moment.

Copy goal: trigger recognition, not persuasion. Don't convince someone to buy. Help them find the one that already belongs to them.

Signals: second person singular ("you", "your"), specific situations not abstract emotions, present tense.

### Collect
Build the opinion wall. Repeat purchase. The "I need the set" feeling.

Copy goal: make buying multiple feel like curation, not spending. You're not buying more stickers — you're building a collection that says something.

Signals: "the set", "your collection", "what's missing", drop language ("new in"), discovery framing.

### Gift
The one that's exactly someone else. "This is literally you, no notes." The organic growth engine — every gift is a new customer.

Copy goal: reduce the friction of recognising and sending. Make it easy to say "this is them." Make it easy to buy without overthinking.

Signals: third person ("them", "they"), gift occasion language, "send this to", "you know who."

## The 5 Voice Rules (Unchanged)

1. **Opinionated.** Have a position. The brand is not neutral.
2. **Group chat register.** Write like you're texting a friend who'll immediately get it.
3. **Edge on humour.** The joke has a point. It's not just silly.
4. **Name the person, not the product.** "For the one who's always right" over "bold graphic sticker."
5. **Short enough to forward on WhatsApp.** If it needs editing to share, it's too long.

## "If someone else likes it, that's a bonus!" — This Line Stays

This is the Find mechanic in one line. Buy it for yourself. It happens to be perfect for someone. That's how word of mouth works here. Do not cut it.

## What "Fun Quirky" Actually Means in Words

**Fun** means: you smile reading it. Not because it's silly — because it's true and the truth is funny.

**Quirky** means: slightly unexpected angle. The obvious approach isn't taken. "Add to cart" becomes "Ship my opinions." The 404 page says something. The order confirmation surprises.

It does NOT mean: random, try-hard, puns for the sake of puns, exclamation marks everywhere, or "hehe" in product copy.

## Product Naming System

### Individual Sticker Names
The name IS the sticker text. "YOUR LOSS" is the product name. No separate descriptor needed.

### Pack / Set Names
Format: "The [Personality]" or a situation the buyer immediately recognises.

Names the person who would buy it, not the contents. Slightly specific is better than broadly appealing.

**Good:**
- "The Loud Ones" — petty/shade pack
- "Pick a Side" — opinion pack
- "Your Energy" — self-aware/relatable pack
- "For the Group Chat" — gifting pack
- "Main Character Set" — aspirational pack
- "No Notes" — gifting pack (perfect as-is)
- "Sent on Read" — relational/situational pack

**Bad (too generic):**
- "Fun Pack" / "Best Seller Bundle" / "Mixed Collection"

## Banned Words and Phrases

- "Fun and quirky" — says nothing specific
- "Express yourself" — too generic
- "Perfect for gifting" — show don't tell
- "High quality" — everyone says this
- "Aesthetic" as a standalone compliment
- "Hehe" / "lol" / "lmao" in product copy
- Excessive exclamation marks (one per page maximum)
- "Curated collection" — overused
- "Are you the kind of person who..." — cliché opener
- "Treat yourself" — use "Treat. Your. Opinions." if needed

---

## The Gifting Mechanic in Copy — How It Spreads

Every time someone buys a gift from TPL, they send a message. The goal is for the product link alone to communicate something specific — no words needed. "THIS IS SO YOU" sent as a link IS the message.

Test for every product description: could someone send this link with no message and still communicate something specific to the recipient? If yes, the copy is working.
