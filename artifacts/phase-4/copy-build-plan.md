## Part 2: Product Description Template

### Before You Write Any PDP, Answer These 3 Questions

Write these down on a scrap of paper before opening Fynd.

**Question 1: Who, specifically, is this for?**
Not "young people who like cool things." One person. What do they do? What do they find funny? What are they tired of? What specific attitude does this product reflect?

**Question 2: What does this product do for that person's sense of self — not its function?**
A keychain holds keys. That is its function. What it does for the person: it says something every time they pull their keys out. What does this specific one say?

**Question 3: What is the one-sentence version that a friend could forward in a WhatsApp message?**
Write this sentence before you write anything else. If you cannot write this sentence, the identity hook is not clear yet.

---

### The Template

Fill every slot. Do not leave any slot empty. Do not add copy outside the structure — if you want to say something, it goes in one of these four sections.

---

**[IDENTITY HOOK]**
For [specific type of person described as attitude, not demographic]. [Optional: one-phrase reinforcement of who this is NOT for, or what situation this is for.]

**[WHY THIS]**
[Name the specific emotional job or social signal this product does for that person.] [One specific material, feature, or design detail that no generic brand would call out because calling it out requires having a point of view.]

**[WHATSAPP LINE — internal label, do not publish this label]**
[One sentence, 15 words or fewer, that works forwarded with no context. No brand name needed. The product speaks for itself.]

**[SPECS]**
[Material]. [Dimensions]. [Finish]. [Variants/colors available]. [Application note if needed — e.g., for card stickers: waterproof, residue-free removal.]

---

### Worked Example 1: Attitude Keychain — "Idiot Repellent"

**Three questions answered first:**
1. For the person who has gotten very good at smiling politely while internally clocking that the conversation is not going anywhere.
2. It validates the quiet internal monologue they have been keeping about the people around them. The humor is the signal — someone who picks this up understands exactly what it means about them.
3. "For the friend who quit small talk and doesn't feel bad about it."

**The description:**

For the person who stopped pretending to enjoy every conversation. You know the ones.

This keychain is not a joke. It is a description. The right person will see it on your bag and immediately know two things about you: that you have opinions, and that you are not embarrassed about them.

*Forward-ready line:* For the friend who quit small talk and doesn't feel bad about it.

Enamel on zinc alloy. 40mm, double-sided. Lobster clasp. Available in single or as part of the No Filter bundle.

---

### Worked Example 2: Card Sticker — "No Chill"

**Three questions answered first:**
1. For the person who has accepted that their neutrality window for nonsense is smaller than average, and they are fine with this.
2. The bank card is the most-used object most people carry. This makes it say something before they open their mouth.
3. "Your bank card says nothing. This fixes that."

**The description:**

For the person whose patience is a finite resource, currently running low.

Your bank card is the one thing you hand to strangers every day. Right now it says: "I have a bank account." This changes that. Stick it on, tap it, and let your card do the talking before you have to.

*Forward-ready line:* Your bank card says nothing. This fixes that.

Premium vinyl. Full-card size (85mm x 55mm). Waterproof, scratch-resistant, residue-free removal. Takes five minutes to apply. Works on all card types.

## The 3 Most Common Mistakes Dan Will Make

### Mistake 1: Starting with the product instead of the person.

**What it looks like:** "Premium vinyl card sticker, waterproof and scratch-resistant, in a range of fun designs."

**What is wrong:** This opens with material and claims "fun" — a banned word — before the customer has any reason to care. The customer is not thinking about waterproofing. She is thinking "is this for someone like me?"

**How to catch it:** Read the first sentence. Does it describe the product or the person? If it describes the product, delete it and start with the identity hook.

---

### Mistake 2: Writing the WhatsApp line last, as an afterthought.

**What it looks like:** The product description is three polished paragraphs, and then at the bottom there is a sentence that summarizes what was already said.

**What is wrong:** The WhatsApp line is not a summary. It is the one sentence that should stop the scroll in a group chat. It needs to be written first — before the full description — because it tells you whether the identity hook is actually clear. If you cannot write a WhatsApp line, the hook is not ready.

**How to catch it:** Write the WhatsApp line before anything else. If writing it feels hard, go back to Question 1 and Question 2.

---

### Mistake 3: Using adjectives where specifics would work.

**What it looks like:** "A beautifully designed keychain for people who love bold accessories."

**What is wrong:** "Beautifully designed," "love," "bold accessories" — all adjectives that say nothing. The brand does not describe itself as bold (banned word). The brand demonstrates it through specific copy.

**How to catch it:** Highlight every adjective in your draft. For each one, ask: what specific detail would replace this? "Beautifully designed" → say what the specific design choice is. "Bold accessories" → name the attitude it signals. If the adjective can be replaced with a specific, replace it. If it cannot, cut it.

## Part 3: Instagram Caption Templates

Three reusable formats. Each has a fill-in structure, a worked example, and a "what not to do" version. The formats are interchangeable — use whichever fits the content.

---

### Format 1: Product Drop Caption

**Structure:**

[One sentence that states the problem the product solves — not as a question, as a fact.]
[One sentence that makes it funny or honest.]

[Product name]. [Fastest-possible description of what it is and what it does.] [Price.]

[Launch/availability info.]
[Link in bio.]

---

**Worked example — Card Sticker drop:**

Your bank card is the most-used object you own.
It still looks like it came with a pamphlet about fraud protection.

Card sticker. Five minutes. Problem solved. ₹149-199.

Live now. Link in bio.

---

**What not to do:**

*"We are SO excited to launch our NEW card stickers! These beautiful, quirky designs are perfect for anyone who loves unique accessories. Shop our curated collection and elevate your wallet game! 🎉 #shopnow #accessories #unique"*

What is wrong: "so excited," "beautiful," "quirky," "unique," "curated," "elevate," all banned. The emoji at the end is fine but the exclamation marks throughout signal a brand that does not trust its own products to speak. The hashtag cluster at the end signals low confidence. None of this is specific to a person.

---

### Format 2: Brand Opinion Caption (No Product)

**Structure:**

[A statement that takes a position about the world the customer lives in. Not about the brand. Not about the product. About something the customer already thinks and has not heard someone say plainly.]

[One sentence that connects the position to what the brand does — or just leaves the position standing on its own.]

[Optional: one parenthetical that makes the brand self-aware without being smug.]

---

**Worked example:**

If someone else likes it, that's a bonus.

(That's all. That's the whole brand.)

---

**Second worked example:**

Your keychain says more about you than your LinkedIn bio.
Your wallet says more about you than your resume.
Your taste in small objects is the most honest thing about you.

---

**What not to do:**

*"At The Product Lab, we believe that self-expression is a journey. We're passionate about helping you find your unique voice through accessories that tell your story. Because everyone deserves to feel seen. ✨"*

What is wrong: "journey" (banned), "passionate" (banned), "unique" (banned), "tell your story" (platform-speak), "feel seen" (every brand says this). The copy is trying to be emotional but is using borrowed language, which makes it ring false. The customer can tell.

---

### Format 3: Repost / Community Caption

**Structure:**

[Customer's words or a paraphrase of what they said — specific, real, not cleaned up too much.]

[One sentence from the brand that adds context without overselling.]

[Optional: one sentence about what this means for the brand, stated plainly — not gratefully, just factually.]

[CTA if needed — link in bio, or none at all.]

---

**Worked example:**

"This is exactly my energy. Already bought a second one as a gift."

That is [Customer name] on the No Chill card sticker. No payment. No obligation. Just: here is something, let us know what you think.

That's the whole pitch. It either is your energy or it isn't.

Link in bio.

---

**What not to do:**

*"WE ARE BLOWN AWAY by this amazing review from our incredible customer! 🥺 This is why we do what we do! Thank you so much for sharing your love with us! 💕"*

What is wrong: The brand is performing gratitude rather than expressing it. "Blown away," "incredible," "this is why we do what we do" — all borrowed phrases that say nothing specific. The emojis are doing emotional labor that the words should do. The customer's actual words are not in the caption. The brand is the hero of this post instead of the customer.

## Part 4: WhatsApp Templates

Copy-paste ready. Adapt only what is in brackets. These are written for the Gupshup/MSG91 broadcast format — short, direct, never formatted with bullet points or markdown.

---

### Template 1: Drop Announcement (New Product)

Hey — new drop just went live.

[Product name(s)] — [one-sentence description of what they are and what they say]. All original designs. All from independent artists.

theproductlab.in

Free shipping on orders above ₹499. [If applicable: First [X] orders get an artist card with their pack.]

That's it.

---

**Dan's notes on this template:**
- Do not open with "Hi" or "Hello" — it signals broadcast, not conversation.
- "That's it" at the close is intentional. It signals confidence. The product is the thing; the copy gets out of the way.
- Update the product name and description each drop. Everything else stays the same.

---

### Template 2: Back in Stock

[Product name] is back.

We restocked [number] units. When they're gone, they're gone — the next restock isn't confirmed yet.

theproductlab.in

---

**Dan's notes:**
- Do not say "finally" or "you asked for it" — these phrases are borrowed from retail brands.
- The quantity note is factual, not manipulative. If you do not know the restock quantity, drop the second sentence and just link.

---

### Template 3: Sale (Without Sounding Like a Discount Brand)

If you've been waiting: ₹[X] off until [day] at [time].

No code. [Works on everything / Works on [specific collection].] Just checkout.

After [day]: full price.

theproductlab.in

---

**Dan's notes:**
- Never lead with the percentage — lead with the product or the mechanic.
- "If you've been waiting" opens with the customer's state of mind, not a promotion.
- "After [day]: full price" is stated as a fact, not as a threat.
- This template is for tactical discounts only. Do not send this more than once per drop cycle.

---

### Template 4: Order Confirmation (if not automated through Fynd)

Hey [Name] — your order is confirmed.

[Product name(s)]. Total: ₹[amount].

We pack and ship within 48 hours. Tracking comes by email when it ships. Most orders: 3-5 days to your city.

Questions? Reply here.

— Dan, The Product Lab

---

**Dan's notes:**
- Only use this if Fynd's automated confirmation is not working or is sending the wrong message.
- "Reply here" is genuine — if they reply, respond within 24 hours.
- Do not add marketing to an order confirmation. The customer has already bought. This message is functional.

## Part 5: The "When Things Go Wrong" Copy Kit

These are the five scenarios most likely to happen in week 1. Each response is written. Dan should read them once before launch so they are in his head, then copy-paste and adapt when needed.

The guiding principle for all of these: a real person is handling a real situation. The brand voice does not disappear under pressure. Tone stays level — not corporate apology, not defensive, not overcompensating with warmth that does not feel earned.

---

### Scenario 1: Customer Complaint on Instagram Comments (Public)

**Situation:** A customer leaves a negative comment on a post. "Ordered last week, nothing arrived yet." Or "Quality is not what I expected."

**Public reply (visible to everyone):**

Hey [Name] — DM us and we'll sort this out. Not ignoring, just easier to fix in private.

---

**Dan's notes:**
- Keep the public response short. Do not explain or defend in public — it reads as arguing.
- "Not ignoring" reassures the customer and anyone watching that this is being handled.
- "Easier to fix in private" is true and signals that the response is practical, not political.
- Move immediately to DM and use Scenario 2 from there.

---

### Scenario 2: Customer Complaint in DMs

**Situation:** A customer is unhappy — delayed order, quality issue, wrong item.

**DM response:**

Hey [Name], thanks for messaging. Tell me exactly what happened — order number if you have it, what you ordered, what went wrong. I'll fix it.

---

**Dan's notes:**
- No "so sorry for the inconvenience." That phrase is the verbal equivalent of an automated response.
- "I'll fix it" is a commitment. Do not say it unless you are prepared to fix it.
- After they reply with details, move to the specific scenario below (delayed, wrong item, etc.).

---

### Scenario 3: Delayed Order

**Situation:** The order is taking longer than the 3-5 day estimate. Dan can confirm this via Shiprocket tracking.

**Response:**

Hey [Name] — your order is running behind. Checking with the carrier now. Here's the tracking: [link]. If it hasn't moved in 24 hours, I'll reship or refund — whichever you prefer. Let me know.

---

**If Dan cannot check tracking immediately:**

Hey [Name] — your order is delayed. I'm looking into exactly where it is right now. Give me until [time] today and I'll have a clear answer for you.

---

**Dan's notes:**
- Give a specific time — "later today" is not a commitment. "[Time] today" is.
- "Reship or refund — whichever you prefer" puts the customer in control. It costs Dan something but it builds trust worth more than the reship.
- Do not blame the carrier by name in the first message. If the carrier is clearly at fault after tracking, you can say "Shiprocket tracking shows it stopped in [city] — we're following up." Factual, not defensive.

---

### Scenario 4: Out of Stock After Order Placed

**Situation:** A customer ordered a product that was showing as in stock but has sold out before their order could be fulfilled.

**Response:**

Hey [Name] — I owe you a straight answer: the [product name] sold out before we could pack your order. That's on us, not on the site.

Two options: I can refund the full amount to your payment method right now. Or I can hold your order at the front of the queue for the restock, which is coming in [timeframe]. Let me know which you prefer and I'll move immediately.

---

**Dan's notes:**
- "That's on us, not on the site" acknowledges the failure without blaming a system.
- Give real options, not vague reassurances. "We'll sort something out" is not an option.
- If you do not have a restock date, say "I don't have a confirmed restock date yet, so if you'd rather not wait, the refund is immediate." Honesty over false comfort.

---

### Scenario 5: Wrong Item Shipped

**Situation:** The customer received a different product than what they ordered.

**Response:**

Hey [Name] — that's a packing error and it's my fault. Here's what I'm going to do: I'll send your correct order [product name] out today or tomorrow, shipping priority. Keep the wrong one — no need to return it. Your original item arrives first, you let me know it did, we're done.

---

**Dan's notes:**
- "Keep the wrong one" removes friction and costs Dan one unit. The alternative — asking them to return it — costs Dan time, the customer goodwill, and creates a second touchpoint for things to go wrong.
- "That's a packing error and it's my fault" — say this even if it was technically a platform error. The customer doesn't need to know whose system failed. They need to know it will be fixed.
- "We're done" at the close signals that you are not going to make them jump through more hoops.
