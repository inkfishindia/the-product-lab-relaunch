## Purpose of This Document

This report updates and deepens the Phase 1 customer insight work in light of the external designer's branding assets for The Product Lab. It answers: who does the designer think this customer is, who the customer actually is, where these assessments diverge, and what that means for Heyward's positioning and Sean's visual identity work.

The designer's assets are treated here as a data source — a set of revealed assumptions about the customer. Those assumptions are compared against the customer evidence established in Phase 1 and the brand strategy locked in Phase 2.

---

## 1. Assessment of the External Designer's Customer Assumption

### What the assets reveal about who the designer thinks the customer is

The designer's Figma work shows a coherent visual language and a set of customer assumptions that can be read directly from the choices made. These are not stated anywhere in the assets — they are inferred from what was built.

**The designer appears to be targeting: a young, art-college-adjacent Indian consumer, 18-25, interested in indie illustration and expressive objects, who responds to bright primary colors, cartoon mascots, and the cultural energy of print-shop / risograph aesthetics.**

Evidence from the assets:

**Visual language signals:** The color palette is built on CMYK primaries — yellow, magenta, cyan, black — with a direct visual analogy to printing and press culture. This is a specific, literate choice. It signals "the production process is part of the identity." The target customer this speaks to is someone who has cultural reference for print culture, knows what a Risograph is, or at minimum responds to the aesthetic vocabulary of indie zines and art print shops. This is a narrower and more culturally specific customer than "young Indian consumer."

**The mascot:** A round-faced illustrated character in a red beanie / cap appears across multiple frames — on keychains, in promotional layouts, as an avatar or brand personality device. This character is warm, approachable, slightly playful, ethnically ambiguous. It reads as Gen Z internet-native: the cartoon avatar as identity stand-in. The customer this assumes is someone who is comfortable with mascot-led brands — someone who grew up with Discord servers, Twitch channels, and brand characters on social media.

**Homepage headline:** "Crafting Comfort, Redefining Spaces. Your Home, Your Signature Style!" — This is a significant tell. The headline conflates accessories, home decor, and apparel into a single statement. The customer this copy addresses is someone looking for a lifestyle destination with broad category coverage: home objects, clothing, accessories together. This is not the focused accessories customer the Phase 2 strategy identified. It implies the designer either did not have a scope brief or chose to imagine a more expansive TPL than the product actually is.

**The varsity jacket as hero product:** A varsity jacket (blue and white, full apparel) is featured prominently in the homepage wireframe as the "Featured Product." This is the most direct evidence of scope confusion. The customer this choice addresses is someone who shops for apparel. Decision D-011 locks TPL to accessories-only at launch. The designer is imagining a customer who does not exist in the current product scope.

**"20% Off Sitewide" banner:** A prominent promotional band runs across the top of the homepage mockup. The customer this addresses is someone whose primary purchase motivation is price. Discount-led homepage architecture is a signal that the designer defaulted to a conversion-maximization assumption — "offer a deal to get them in the door." This conflicts with the brand territory of "small objects, big opinions," where the purchase motivation is identity, not savings.

**"Showcase your art to the world and earn money doing what you love":** This copy appears in artist-facing poster collateral. The customer being addressed here is the artist-creator, not the buyer. The designer has assigned significant visual real estate to the B2B supply side of the brand (artist recruitment) rather than the B2C demand side (the buyer experience). This is a strategic emphasis choice, and it is worth flagging: the artist story should inform the buyer story, not replace it.

**"If someone else likes it, that's a bonus!":** This is the one piece of copy in the designer's assets that is exactly right. It captures the "I buy this for me, not for external validation" identity-first motivation that Weiss and Heyward have both identified as the core purchase driver. The designer found this insight but did not build the rest of the visual language to match it.

**Collective Cards / STORMS series:** A series of collectible cards with themes ("Storms," "Storms Within," "Bolt") appears in one frame. These appear to be art+psychology+spirituality themed cards — more Tarot-adjacent than keychain-adjacent. The customer this imagines is someone interested in introspection, wellness aesthetics, and art-object collecting. This is a valid adjacent customer but not the core customer TPL's product set actually serves.

**Artist community reference images:** Three artist work samples appear under "Your Ticket to Join Our Artist Community" — illustrative poster art (Fauna), layered graphic collage work, and Mr. Doodle-style black and white doodle art. These are sophisticated, gallery-adjacent art references. The customer implied here has fine art or design school context. This is a segment that exists in India (Bengaluru specifically) but is a narrower slice than the mass-accessible Gen Z buyer TPL needs to reach to hit its growth targets.

### Summary of the designer's assumed customer

The designer is imagining a creative-class Indian young adult who has art school or design culture exposure, responds to print-culture aesthetics, collects objects with illustration value, may make art themselves, and wants a lifestyle destination brand that covers their expressive objects, home, and clothing in one place. This is a real person — but it is not the primary customer TPL needs to reach at launch, and the scope implied (home + apparel + accessories) does not match the locked product scope (accessories only).

## 2. Actual Customer Segments

These segments are built on the Phase 1 evidence base, the Phase 2 brand strategy (locked decisions D-005, D-006, D-011), and the additional signal provided by the designer's assets. The designer's work adds one useful refinement: the creative-class customer is a real segment that the visual language should be legible to, but it cannot be the only person the brand speaks to.

### Segment 1: The Self-Expression Buyer (Primary)

**Name used internally:** The Loud Whisper.

**Who they are:** 19-26, urban India (Bengaluru, Mumbai, Delhi NCR, Pune, Hyderabad, Chandigarh, Jaipur). Entry-level job or final years of college. Monthly income ₹0-40,000 — discretionary spend is limited but allocated intentionally. Android-first. Instagram and WhatsApp are their primary channels. They follow illustrators, meme accounts, subculture communities (K-Pop, anime, sneakers, indie music), and small Indian brands they discovered through Reels.

**Their relationship with objects:** Objects are not decorative to this customer — they are communicative. A keychain on a bag, a sticker on a laptop, a card sticker in a wallet: these are small public broadcasts about taste. They do not want to explain what they are into; they want an object that does that explaining for them. They are not shopping for "accessories." They are shopping for something that says the right thing about who they are.

**What makes them buy vs. scroll past:** They buy when they see a product and think "this is so me" before they think about price. They scroll past when a product is clearly for everyone — when it has no specific personality. They scroll past promotional copy. They stop for a design that makes them feel recognized.

**Price sensitivity:** At ₹149, they buy without much deliberation if the design resonates. At ₹249, they may share it with a friend on WhatsApp before buying. At ₹499, the product needs a story — why is this worth ₹499 — or it needs to be a bundle that feels like more than its components.

**What they do not want:** They do not want to be addressed as a bargain hunter. They do not want "fun and quirky." They do not want a brand that is also trying to sell them a varsity jacket and a house plant and a mug. They want a brand that knows exactly what it sells and is very good at it.

**What the designer's assets get right for them:** The CMYK color palette and print-shop aesthetic would resonate with a subset of this segment — the more design-literate end. The mascot character may feel too juvenile for some of them. The "If someone else likes it, that's a bonus!" copy is perfect for them. The varsity jacket and the "Redefining Spaces. Your Home" messaging does nothing for them and may actively confuse them.

---

### Segment 2: The Thoughtful Gifter (Secondary)

**Name used internally:** The Gifter Who Wants Credit for Knowing You.

**Who they are:** 22-30, often the same person as Segment 1 but in a different purchase mode. Working professional, 1-5 years into their career, ₹25,000-80,000/month. They are buying for someone they know well — a best friend's birthday, Raksha Bandhan, a college friend's going-away. The quality of their gift choice reflects on them socially.

**Their relationship with objects:** When buying for someone else, the product is a stand-in for "I know you specifically." They need the object to communicate something about the recipient's personality. Generic products — even attractive ones — fail this test because they cannot be given as proof of knowledge.

**What makes them buy vs. scroll past:** They buy when the product or the product's framing helps them answer: "why did I pick this for this specific person?" A product with a personality — "for the friend who peaked in college and knows it," "for the one who always has too many browser tabs open" — is easier to give than a beautiful object with no described point of view. They scroll past collections with no editorial curation, because curation means someone has already done the interpretive work for them.

**Price sensitivity:** The ₹299-499 range is where this customer's gifting intent peaks. At ₹399-499, a bundle feels like a "real gift." At ₹249 for a single item, they often add a second product to make it feel more substantial. Free shipping at ₹499 is a significant AOV driver for this customer.

**What the designer's assets give them:** Almost nothing, currently. The designer's homepage is organized around "View Our Range of Categories" — a product-type navigation. This does not help the gifter, who is not thinking in product types. They need occasion-based or personality-based collections. The designer has not built this framing.

---

### Segment 3: The Collector (Tertiary)

**Name used internally:** The Micro-Collector.

**Who they are:** 17-24, likely male-skewing (60/40), subculture-defined. Anime, K-Pop, gaming, sneakers, vinyl/pin collecting. Student or early career. Small budget, high intention. They follow drops. They buy to complete sets or own something scarce.

**Their relationship with objects:** They display what they own. The pin board, the sticker grid on a laptop, the keychain ring — these are curated exhibitions of taste and community membership. They are not buying to communicate to strangers; they are buying to participate in a community of people who recognize the specific reference.

**What makes them buy vs. scroll past:** Scarcity and originality. They need to believe the design is not available everywhere else and that the run is actually limited. "Limited drop — 50 made" with no visible countdown or sold-out badge is unconvincing. The social proof from their community — other collectors sharing purchases — is more valuable than any brand marketing.

**Price sensitivity:** At ₹149-249, they buy impulse. At ₹349, they need to believe in the scarcity. At ₹499+, the product needs to be a set or a special edition.

**What the designer's assets give them:** The "Collective Cards" STORMS series is interesting for a collector — but it appears to be targeting a different content theme (psychology/wellness) that does not align with this customer's subculture interests. The illustrative character designs on keychains could work for them if the character has subculture resonance.

## 3. Primary Persona — The Loud Whisper (Deep Profile)

**Name:** Meghna (composite)
**Age:** 23
**City:** Bengaluru, living in a shared flat in Koramangala
**Life stage:** First job, 14 months in. UX designer at a mid-size startup. Earning ₹35,000/month. About ₹5,000-8,000 in discretionary monthly spend after rent and food.

### Who she is

Meghna graduated with a design degree, which means she has a vocabulary for visual culture that most of her friends do not. She notices typefaces. She knows what a Risograph print is. She follows Indian illustrators on Instagram — Santanu Hazarika, Rajiv Surendra, smaller Bengaluru-based artists she discovered through algorithmic rabbit holes. She has opinions about design, but she does not perform those opinions loudly. She expresses them through what she chooses to put on herself and her things.

Her laptop has seven stickers on it, each chosen deliberately. Two of them she bought at a Design Yatra pop-up. One she got from a friend who visited Japan. The rest she found through Instagram. No brand loyalty — she goes where the design is right. Her keychain has an enamel pin and a small charm from a ceramics seller she found on Instagram. Her bank card has a sticker on it — she put it on because she liked the design and she thought it was funny that a debit card could look like something other than a debit card.

She does not think of herself as a "collector." She thinks of herself as someone with taste.

### Her relationship with art and design

She follows independent illustrators more than brands. She buys from those illustrators — a print, a postcard, occasionally something from their store. She values original work. She can tell, usually, when a design was made by someone who cared about it versus a design produced for maximum shelf appeal.

She respects small brands that have a coherent point of view. She is suspicious of brands that seem to be everything to everyone. When she finds a brand that feels specific — "this is for people like me, specifically" — she becomes a low-key evangelist. She shares it in her friend group chat. She tags it in her Stories. Not for the brand's benefit, but because recommending something good is a social act in her world.

She has been burned once by a pretty-looking product that arrived looking cheap. Since then, she looks for material descriptions, zoom-in photos, and any evidence that other real people have bought this and been satisfied.

### How she discovers products

Primarily Instagram Reels. The algorithm has correctly understood that she is interested in Indian illustration and small-brand products. She also discovers through her friend group — a WhatsApp thread of six people who share things they find. If one of her friends sends a product link in that thread, she will look at it seriously, even if she would have scrolled past it organically.

She does not discover via Google search. She does not browse Flipkart. She does not respond to generic promotional emails unless the subject line is arresting. She has never bought something because of a banner ad.

### What makes her buy versus scroll past

**She buys when:**
- The design makes her think "I need this on my bag" before she checks the price
- The product name or description gives her something to laugh about or feel recognized by ("Bullshit Remover" would stop her scroll)
- A friend has sent her the link with "this is so you"
- The product photography looks like the object lives in a real, specific world — not a white background with an SKU number
- The price is under ₹300 and the design is right, she barely hesitates

**She scrolls past when:**
- The copy says "quirky accessories" or "unique designs" — she has learned these words mean nothing
- The product photography looks generic, AI-processed, or overproduced
- The homepage is leading with a discount banner — it signals the brand does not believe its own products are worth full price
- The product range is incoherent — if she cannot figure out what the brand is for in 8 seconds, she leaves
- The brand voice is corporate or generic — "Explore our curated collection of premium lifestyle accessories" would lose her immediately

**At ₹149:** Buys without much deliberation if the design is right. Will probably add another item to hit free shipping.
**At ₹249:** Considers briefly, may share to WhatsApp to get a second opinion. Buys if validation comes back.
**At ₹499:** Needs the product to feel like a statement or a small ritual. A bundle at ₹499 she can justify more easily than a single ₹499 item. The free shipping threshold at ₹499 is an active motivator.

### Jobs to be done

**Functional:** Put something on my bag/laptop/wallet that represents my taste without having to explain it.
**Emotional:** Feel like there is a brand that gets me — not in a pandering way, but in a "this was made by someone who exists in the same cultural space as me" way.
**Social:** When someone notices what she is wearing or carrying and asks about it, she wants to be able to say "it's from this small brand from Bengaluru" and have that feel like a good answer.

## 5. What the External Designer Got Right and Wrong

This section is a direct assessment of specific creative choices against the customer evidence. Evidence leads. Interpretation is labeled as such.

### What the designer got right

**1. "If someone else likes it, that's a bonus!" — exactly right.**
This is the most precise insight the designer surfaced, and it is identical to the core emotional job identified in the Phase 1 and Phase 2 research. The customer buys for themselves, not for external validation. This copy should be preserved and elevated — it is the most customer-accurate language in the entire asset set.

**2. The CMYK color approach speaks to the right aesthetic territory.**
Print culture, bold primary colors, the visual language of handmade and indie-produced goods — this resonates with the design-literate end of the primary segment. It is not wrong. It may be too narrow (art school in-crowd rather than broader youth culture), but the instinct is defensible. The existing locked visual system (Darkroom + Type Pressure, #1A1A1A, #E63B2E, #F2D024) overlaps in energy: bold, unapologetic, graphic. The designer was working in adjacent territory.

**3. The artist marketplace framing as a brand identity pillar.**
"Showcase your art to the world and earn money doing what you love" is the right message for artist recruitment, and the visual space given to artist-community positioning is appropriate for a brand that wants "made by independent artists" to be a trust signal for buyers. The designer understood that the supply side (artists) and the demand side (buyers) are connected brand stories.

**4. The mascot character as brand personality device.**
Having a brand mascot is a legitimate choice for this audience — Gen Z responds to character-based branding, and the cartoon figure appears consistently across the designer's assets, giving the brand a face. The character concept is valid even if the specific execution may need refinement to fit the locked visual system.

**5. The collectible card series concept (STORMS / STORMS WITHIN / BOLT).**
A card-based collectible series — numbered, with a thematic universe — is a product format that directly serves the collector segment's completionist behavior. The specific theme chosen (psychology/wellness/spirituality) may not align with the TPL catalog, but the product format logic is correct and worth retaining as a concept.

**6. Product range includes card stickers.**
The designer included card stickers in the product range visual — this is the most differentiated TPL product, confirmed unique in the competitive set, and the designer has it in the range. Good.

---

### What the designer got wrong

**1. "Crafting Comfort, Redefining Spaces. Your Home, Your Signature Style!" — wrong on multiple levels.**

This headline is wrong in three separate ways:

- It is out of scope. "Your Home" implies home decor. TPL is accessories-only at launch (D-011). The designer is selling a product the brand does not carry.
- "Crafting Comfort" is Chumbak language — warm, domestic, lifestyle-magazine register. The TPL brand territory is opinionated and specific, not comfortable and warm. These are opposite registers.
- The sentence structure is flaccid. "Redefining Spaces" is corporate marketing language with zero emotional charge. Compare to "Small objects. Big opinions." — which is locked, locked for a reason, and could not be more different from what the designer wrote.

This is not a small copy error. The homepage headline is the first thing the primary customer reads. Getting it wrong means the customer — who is pattern-matching in 3 seconds to decide whether to stay — sees a brand that is not for them and leaves.

**2. The varsity jacket as hero product — scope error with customer consequences.**

A blue-and-white varsity jacket is featured as the "Featured Product" on the designer's homepage wireframe. This product category is out of scope (D-011) and directs customer attention toward apparel purchase intent. The primary customer is not coming to TPL for a varsity jacket. Surfacing apparel as the hero product would confuse every customer who is actually in the market for accessories.

**3. "20% Off Sitewide" as the dominant homepage visual element.**

A pink promotional band advertising 20% off sitewide runs across the top of the homepage wireframe, repeated three times across the width. This is the most visible element on the above-the-fold experience.

The customer evidence is unambiguous on this: the primary customer does not buy because of discounts. She buys because the design is right. Leading with a discount banner signals that the brand does not believe its products are worth full price — this is exactly the trust-erosion pattern Heyward identified as a positioning failure mode. The brand's emotional job is "feel like your taste is validated" — a sitewide discount banner is the opposite of that.

There is also a commercial reason this is wrong: at the ₹149-249 price point, a 20% discount means ₹30-50 off. The customer does not feel meaningfully richer. But the brand feels meaningfully cheaper.

**4. Homepage navigation: Home / Contact / Blog — no product architecture.**

The nav shown in the wireframe is Home, Contact, Blog. There is no visible collection or category navigation. For a first-time visitor who has arrived from Instagram and wants to browse, this navigation provides no path into the product. The customer needs a way to explore "what does this brand make" within the first interaction. The Phase 3 UX work (Kurt) has already resolved this correctly — the designer's nav regression would damage the discovery journey.

**5. The product range image includes apparel (t-shirt, sweatshirt) alongside accessories.**

Beyond the varsity jacket, the product range visual shows a white t-shirt with a character illustration and a black sweatshirt with a donut graphic. These are out-of-scope products. More specifically, they dilute the brand's sharpness. If TPL launches claiming to be an accessories brand but shows apparel in its product range, the customer cannot build a clear mental model of what the brand is for. Scope discipline is customer clarity.

**6. The Collective Cards series theme (psychology/wellness) does not match the actual customer.**

"STORMS / STORMS WITHIN / BOLT — art + psychology + spiritualism + wellness" is a coherent product concept, but for a different customer: the journaling / self-help adjacent buyer, not the attitude-and-humor accessories buyer that TPL's existing catalog actually serves. TPL's most distinct products are things like "Bullshit Remover" and "Idiot Repellent" keychains — the emotional register is irreverent, not introspective. A wellness-themed collectible card series requires a brand positioning and buyer motivation that does not currently exist in TPL's product or audience foundation.

**7. The "write up" copy treatment — lorem ipsum with "SED UT PERSPCIATIS UNDE" as a headline placeholder.**

Minor but worth noting: the typography frame labeled "Font Pairing" uses lorem ipsum Latin placeholder with no TPL-specific copy. This means the font selection was made without considering how the brand's actual voice ("Small objects. Big opinions." / "Bullshit Remover" / "Wear your opinion.") reads in those typefaces. Typography selected without testing the actual brand voice can produce a visual system that looks good on dummy text and feels wrong on real copy.

## 6. Insight Handoff for Heyward and Sean

These are the five non-negotiable customer insights that must survive the rebrand. They are stated in customer terms, not brand aspiration terms.

### Insight 1: The customer buys to feel specific, not to feel included.

The primary purchase driver is not belonging to a community or celebrating shared culture (that is The Souled Store's job). It is feeling that a brand sees you specifically — your particular sense of humor, your particular taste, your particular frustrations. "If someone else likes it, that's a bonus" is not casual copy. It is a customer truth. Every visual and copy decision that makes TPL feel more broadly welcoming makes it less useful to the customer it actually needs.

**Implication for Sean:** The visual system should feel like it was made for a specific kind of person, not for everyone who likes color and illustration. "Opinionated" means the visual system takes positions. It should make some people feel excluded — that exclusion is a feature.

**Implication for Heyward:** The brand message cannot be optimized for the widest possible audience. Precision attracts. Breadth repels.

---

### Insight 2: The homepage headline is the hardest-working customer communication the brand produces.

The designer's headline — "Crafting Comfort, Redefining Spaces" — demonstrates what happens when the visual system is correct but the verbal communication is absent. The customer in the target segment will read that headline in under three seconds and make a stay/leave decision. The locked headline "Small objects. Big opinions." is not a tagline preference — it is a customer retention device. It answers the customer's first question ("is this for me?") in four words.

**Implication for Sean:** The headline must be integrated into the visual system at the highest possible prominence. It is not body copy. It is the brand's loudest signal.

**Implication for Heyward:** Any copy from the designer's assets that does not pass the "would a TPL customer feel recognized by this?" test must be replaced before build.

---

### Insight 3: The product range images must match the brand scope exactly — no apparel, no home decor.

From the evidence: the customer builds their mental model of what a brand is in the first 8-12 seconds of a visit. If the first images they see include a varsity jacket, their model of TPL becomes "also an apparel brand." This is scope confusion that costs them clarity, and clarity is what converts first-time visitors into first-time buyers. The customer who came for a keychain that says something about who they are is not helped by a sweatshirt — even a good one.

**Implication for Sean:** Hero product photography and product range imagery in the visual system must feature only the actual product categories at launch: keychains, lapel pins, card stickers, stickers, magnets, earrings. No exceptions for aesthetic variety.

**Implication for Heyward:** The brand position "accessories with opinions" only holds if every customer touchpoint — including what products are shown — reinforces accessories as the category.

---

### Insight 4: Promotional framing destroys the value case before the customer has made one.

The primary customer is not price-motivated at the ₹149-249 range. She is design-motivated. A sitewide discount banner above the fold does two things: it signals the brand does not trust its products to sell at full price, and it draws her attention toward savings rather than identity. The moment she is thinking about the percentage off, she has stopped thinking about whether the design is right for her. The customer you want to attract is not looking for 20% off a ₹149 keychain. She is looking for a ₹149 keychain that is exactly, specifically right.

**Implication for Sean:** No promotional banners in the primary visual treatment. If discount mechanics exist (prepaid discount ₹30, per D-006), they belong in cart or checkout, not above the fold.

**Implication for Heyward:** The pricing architecture (D-006) is already correct for this customer. The implementation must not undercut it by making the first brand impression a discount announcement.

---

### Insight 5: The gifter is the same person as the buyer — the site must serve both modes simultaneously without serving neither.

The Thoughtful Gifter is not a different demographic. She is Meghna on her best friend's birthday. She needs the same product she would buy for herself — just organized and framed to help her justify the choice to someone else. This means the site needs an occasion or personality layer on top of the product layer. Not a separate "gifts" section buried in the footer — a way of reading the catalog that answers "what do I get for someone who is like X."

The designer's "product range" framing is organized by object type (keychains, card stickers, apparel). This serves the buyer who already knows what they want. It does not serve the gifter who knows who she is buying for but not what. Both customers exist. The first customer is self-directed; the second customer needs interpretive help that the brand must provide.

**Implication for Kurt / Andy (via this report):** Collection architecture should include at least one personality-based or occasion-based entry point, not only product-type navigation.

**Implication for Heyward:** The messaging hierarchy should give gifting equal presence to self-purchase — both on the homepage and in campaign framing.

## 7. Evidence Gaps — Updated for Phase 4

These gaps were present in Phase 1 and remain unresolved. They are flagged here because Phase 4 (build) is the last point before launch at which they can be addressed.

| Gap | Current Status | Why It Matters Now |
|-----|---------------|--------------------|
| Zero direct customer voice from TPL buyers | Unresolved | The gifting campaign and product copy cannot be validated without knowing what actual buyers respond to. Dan's 20-gift seeding plan (D-007) is the only mechanism to generate this before launch. |
| Which products are actually selling | Unresolved | The designer correctly identifies keychains and card stickers as hero products, but this is assumption not data. If any Accounts Wizard order history is accessible, it should be consulted before the hero product decision is finalized. |
| Instagram engagement rate | Unresolved | 2,943 followers is a starting point. What the engagement rate is on recent posts determines whether the current audience can be converted to launch traffic or whether the account effectively needs to be rebuilt. |

## 8. What I Am Not Claiming

- I am not claiming the designer's visual language is wrong — only that it is attached to the wrong scope and the wrong headline. The aesthetic instincts (bold, print-culture, CMYK, character-based) are defensible for the target audience.
- I am not claiming the mascot character should be discarded. I am saying its reception is unknown and the decision to anchor the brand to it should be made with customer input.
- I am not claiming the collectible card series is a bad product idea. I am saying the theme chosen does not match the current TPL customer evidence.
- I am not claiming the gifter segment is larger than the self-expression segment. I am saying they are the same person in different modes, and the site must serve both without choosing one over the other.
- I am not claiming the designer was working without any brief. I am observing what the assets reveal about the brief they appear to have been given — which includes apparel, home decor, and a promotional discount architecture that conflicts with the locked positioning.
