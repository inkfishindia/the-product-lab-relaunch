## Merchandising Diagnosis

The Product Lab's catalog has a structural contradiction. It contains 300-400 SKUs but no hierarchy. Every product is equally visible and equally invisible. A first-time visitor cannot tell in ten seconds what this brand is for, what it does best, or why they should buy anything over the dozens of other options in this category. This is not a product problem. It is a merchandising problem.

The classification work here is driven by a single filter drawn from Heyward's positioning: **does this product have an opinion?** If it does, it belongs in the hero tier — it is brand-defining, it earns the "small objects, big opinions" claim, and it gets prime real estate. If it amplifies a hero or helps a customer build an order without defining the brand, it is support. If it could exist on any generic accessories site — or worse, on Planet Superheroes with a license that outranks us — it is filler.

A second filter drives collection and bundle logic: **does this serve at least one of the three named audience segments?** The Loud Whisper (self-expression buyer), The Thoughtful Gifter, and The Micro-Collector each have distinct discovery paths and purchase motivations. Every collection must be designed for one of them as a primary audience.

### Current state problems this document addresses

- No collection architecture: the 40+ theme taxonomy exists only as a hidden product filter, invisible to first-time visitors
- No hero product designation: all 300-400 SKUs are treated as equal
- No bundles or gift sets: AOV is stuck below ₹249, which Patrick has established is unsustainable
- Licensed IP and original designs share equal real estate, creating brand incoherence
- Cross-format design families (same design available as keychain, magnet, earring) are siloed by product type and never merchandised as collections
- No scarcity signals, no editorial framing, no "best sellers" or "new arrivals"

### What this document decides

1. Which product types and themes are hero, support, or filler
2. What the ten launch-day collections are, who they serve, and what goes in them
3. What bundles to create and at what price points
4. How to handle licensed IP products on the new store
5. What the drop framework looks like for the Micro-Collector segment

## Task 2.4 — Hero / Support / Filler Classification

### Classification Logic

The classification applies three tests in order:

**Test 1 — Opinion test (Heyward):** Does this product express a point of view, attitude, or identity beyond its physical form? A "Bullshit Remover" keychain passes. A generic food-themed magnet does not.

**Test 2 — Differentiation test (Maria):** Does a better-resourced competitor own this product type or design territory more credibly? Star Wars lapel pins fail this test — Planet Superheroes has official licensing. Card stickers pass — no competitor offers them.

**Test 3 — AOV test (Patrick):** Can this product anchor or contribute to an order above ₹399? Heroes and support products must map to the ₹249-399 core range or the ₹499-999 bundle tier.

### Tier 1: Hero Products

**Definition:** These products ARE the brand. They get homepage placement, primary collection slots, lifestyle photography investment, Instagram content priority, and first-position on every relevant page. A first-time visitor should be able to identify these within ten seconds of landing.

---

#### Hero 1: Card Stickers (Debit / Credit Card Skins)

**Rationale:** Unique product type. No competitor in the identified competitive set offers card skins for ATM/debit cards. This is confirmed in Maria's catalog audit (Observation 4) and competitor research (Observation 3). The product lives in the wallet — it is the single most intimate physical object the target customer carries. It has inherent viral mechanics: the card reveal moment is shareable. The half-card / full-card variant structure is already in place. The product physically embodies the brand idea — an opinionated object that most people leave generic. There is no stronger proof of "small objects, big opinions" in the catalog.

**Which themes belong here:** All card sticker designs qualify as hero, because the product type itself is differentiating. However, priority placement goes to attitude/humor designs and identity-signal designs (not licensed IP themes like Cartoon Network or Friends card stickers, which are support tier).

**Site placement:**
- Position 1 on homepage hero section
- Standalone featured collection: "Your Card, Your Opinion" (see Collection 3 below)
- First product category in site navigation after homepage
- Badge on product tiles: "Only at The Product Lab"

**Photography investment:** Card sticker lifestyle photography is the single highest-priority creative brief for Sean. The product needs in-context imagery showing the card in a wallet, being tapped at a reader, being handed over. The reveal moment is the content.

**Pricing:** Remain in ₹149-199 range for individual stickers. This is the entry price point that enables impulse trial. Upsell to multi-sticker packs at ₹399-499 (see bundle section).

---

#### Hero 2: Attitude / Humor Collection (Cross-Category)

**Rationale:** The naming system "Bullshit Remover," "Idiot Repellent," "Enjoy the ShitShow," "Straight outta F#_ks to give" is the clearest evidence of a brand voice in the entire catalog. This is not a product type — it is an attitude franchise. These products exist across multiple formats (keychain, magnet, earring) and should be merchandised together as a collection defined by attitude, not by product type. Heyward's positioning brief is explicit: "Classify attitude/humor products as Tier 1 regardless of category — the attitude is the collection, not the product type."

This collection directly serves The Loud Whisper (self-expression buyer). The voice is adult, unsanitized, and specific. These are the products that create word-of-mouth: someone sends their friend a photo of the "Idiot Repellent" keychain, the friend laughs, the friend buys one.

**Which specific products belong here:**
- "Bullshit Remover" keychain
- "Idiot Repellent" keychain
- "Enjoy the ShitShow" (keychain, magnet, earring variants — all formats)
- "Straight outta F#_ks to give" keychain
- Any other product with humor/attitude naming that passes the opinion test (to be confirmed when Andy reviews full SKU list)
- Any product in the Sassy or Humor & Memes theme tags that has original, non-licensed design

**Site placement:**
- Standalone collection: "No Filter" (see Collection 1 below)
- Featured section on homepage: second position after card stickers
- Cross-linked from individual product pages within the collection
- Instagram priority: product names are themselves content

**Photography investment:** Lifestyle photography showing the product on a real person's keychain, bag, or desk — in context. The humor must read at a glance. No sterile white-background shots as primary images for this collection.

**Pricing:** Keychains at ₹249 (repriced from current ₹149-199 per Patrick's recommendation). Magnets at ₹199. Earrings at ₹249. The attitude franchise supports a modest price premium over non-attitude products in the same category.

#### Hero 3: Original-Design Lapel Pins (Curated Selection)

**Rationale:** 101 SKUs in the lapel pins category. Not all of them are heroes — licensed IP pins (Spiderman, Darth Vader, Coca-Cola, Rick & Morty) are filler. But the original-design lapel pins are the brand's strongest collectible product. They are wearable, portable, and the format most associated with collector culture. With 94% of Gen Z and millennials expressing interest in collectibles (Grand View Research, via Maria), and with the Micro-Collector segment defined by pin-board and set-completion behavior, original lapel pins are the primary vehicle for the drop framework.

The lapel pin's physical format — small, displayable, wearable — maps perfectly to the brand idea. Every pin is an opinion in 35mm. The artist attribution model makes pins the natural format for artist series: a named artist, a set of 5 pins, a limited run, a sold-out badge.

**Which themes belong here (from the 40+ theme taxonomy):** Adventure, Anime, Art & Design, Books, Dogs, Gaming (original game-culture designs, not licensed game IP), Music, Nature, Sneakerheads, Sports (culture, not club logos), Travel, Typography — these themes have the highest original-design potential and the clearest identity-signal value for The Loud Whisper and Micro-Collector audiences.

**Which themes to deprioritize within pins:** Harry Potter, Star Wars, Pokemon, K-Pop (if the designs are licensed character reproductions rather than original artist takes on those fandoms). See licensed IP section below.

**Site placement:**
- Artist series drops: dedicated drop pages with artist name, story, and limited quantity signal
- Permanent collection: "The Pin Edit" (see Collection 5 below) — rotating curation of 20-30 original pins
- New Arrivals section should feature new pin drops prominently

**Photography investment:** Pins need both flat product shots (for catalog accuracy) and lifestyle shots showing pins on jacket lapels, tote bags, backpacks. The "pin board" aesthetic — a corkboard or fabric surface with an arrangement of pins — is proven social content that The Micro-Collector audience will reshare.

**Pricing:** Original-design lapel pins repriced to ₹249-299 (from current ₹149-199 range). Artist collaboration pins in limited drops: ₹349. Sets of 3 pins: ₹699.

### Tier 2: Support Products

**Definition:** These products complement heroes, drive AOV through add-ons and bundles, fill out the brand world, and serve occasions the hero products do not anchor. They get collection placement but not homepage hero slots. They are not marketed independently — they are marketed as part of hero collections or as gifting add-ons.

---

#### Support 1: Identity / Interest Keychains (Non-Attitude Themes)

**What they are:** Keychains themed around identity and subculture — K-Pop, Anime, Music, Typography, Art & Design, Tech, Travel, Sneakerheads — where the design is original (not licensed) but the primary appeal is interest-identity rather than attitude/humor.

**Why support and not hero:** These products serve the same customer as the attitude/humor collection but with a softer message. "I'm into K-Pop" is an identity signal, but it is not as opinionated as "Enjoy the ShitShow." These keychains are the logical add-on to a hero purchase — the Loud Whisper customer who buys the "No Filter" attitude keychain may also buy a music-themed keychain. They are AOV builders.

**Site placement:** Within theme collections (K-Pop Drop, Sneakerhead Edit, etc. — see Collection architecture below). Cross-linked from attitude collection as "More from your vibe." Not on homepage hero.

**Pricing:** ₹199-249.

---

#### Support 2: Fridge Magnets (Original Designs)

**What they are:** Original-design fridge magnets covering attitude, humor, and lifestyle themes. "Enjoy the ShitShow" magnet, "Sneaker Collection" magnet, and others.

**Why support and not hero:** Fridge magnets are a lower-impulse gift item. They are not wearable or carryable — they sit at home. This reduces their identity-signaling power (the core driver for The Loud Whisper). However, they are excellent gifting products for The Thoughtful Gifter (a magnet for a friend's new apartment, a first-job, a hostel room) and good AOV-building add-ons to pin and keychain orders. The "Enjoy the ShitShow" magnet belongs in the No Filter attitude collection.

**Site placement:** Within relevant theme or occasion collections. Appear as add-ons in bundles. Not standalone hero.

**Pricing:** ₹199 (repriced from ₹109-149). The current pricing is below sustainable threshold per Patrick.

---

#### Support 3: Laptop Stickers

**What they are:** Sticker packs for laptop decoration, confirmed at ₹225 current price.

**Why support and not hero:** Laptop stickers are a category where Dot Badges competes aggressively on price. TPL's differentiator in stickers is original design and brand coherence — not price or volume. Laptop stickers serve the Loud Whisper audience (laptop personalization is a direct identity-signaling behavior) but they do not define the brand the way card stickers do. They are a natural add-on when someone buys pins or card stickers.

**Site placement:** In theme collections (same design themes as pins and keychains where cross-format consistency exists). In "Complete the Look" bundles. Not hero position.

**Pricing:** ₹225 current. Move individual stickers to ₹199 (accessible trial), packs of 3-5 stickers to ₹499 (AOV-driving). This keeps the per-sticker price consistent while creating a bundle-friendly product tier.

#### Support 4: Earrings (Original Designs, Curated Selection)

**What they are:** The earrings range — "Enjoy the Shit Show," "Cute Peach," "Gin," "Ice Cream Love Bites," "Burger," "Unicorn," "Rainbow," "Vacation in a Bottle" — shares the brand's irreverent DNA.

**Why support and not hero:** Earrings require a different purchasing context than the other categories. They are wearable jewelry, which raises the quality-verification bar. The current catalog has no lifestyle photography for earrings (Maria, Section 3.4). Without lifestyle photography showing the earrings worn, they cannot perform as heroes. However, the "Enjoy the ShitShow" earring belongs in the No Filter attitude collection — cross-format coherence is a brand asset.

**Promotion path to hero status:** If Sean delivers lifestyle photography of earrings worn (face/ear context), and if the earrings prove to sell above threshold in months 1-3 post-relaunch, elevate the curated earring selection to hero status in Phase 4 or 5. Specifically the humor/attitude earrings have hero potential.

**Site placement:** "Enjoy the ShitShow" earring appears in the No Filter collection alongside keychain and magnet variants. Other earrings appear in the "She's Got an Opinion" gifting collection. Earrings do not have a standalone navigation category at launch — they appear within relevant collections. The existing "Earrings" nav category should remain for users who specifically search by product type, but collection placement drives discovery.

**Pricing:** ₹249 (repriced from current unconfirmed). Must be positioned as a purposeful gift, not a commodity. Matches lapel pin pricing to signal equivalent quality.

#### Support 5: 3D Pop-Up Stickers

**What they are:** A sticker subcategory confirmed as existing (3D Pop Up Stickers under the Stickers nav). Specific products and prices not confirmed from Phase 1 data.

**Why support:** Unconfirmed product details prevent hero classification. Andy should surface this category fully in Phase 4. If 3D pop-up stickers contain strong original designs at appropriate prices, they have collection potential. Until confirmed, classify as support.

**Site placement:** Within relevant theme collections where designs align. Not standalone.

### Tier 3: Filler Products

**Definition:** Keep for now but deprioritize. Do not invest marketing spend, photography, or prime site placement in these products. Do not feature them in launches or drops. Review for phase-out after three months of post-relaunch data if they are not converting. Some may be retained for catalog depth (the "long tail" that search traffic finds) without being actively promoted.

---

#### Filler 1: Licensed IP Products (All Formats)

**What they are:** Any product featuring Marvel, DC, Star Wars, Pokemon, Disney, Rick & Morty, Friends, BTS, Cartoon Network, Game of Thrones, or any other licensed intellectual property as the primary design element.

**Why filler:**
- These products do not support the positioning. "Small objects, big opinions" requires original opinion. A Darth Vader keychain is someone else's opinion — George Lucas's, technically Disney's now.
- Planet Superheroes, The Souled Store, Hamee India, and Redwolf all compete in licensed IP with official licensing. TPL's licensed products (if not officially licensed) are legally precarious. If licensed, they cost margin. Either way, they are competing against better-resourced brands on the licensed brand's terms.
- Heyward's positioning brief is explicit: "Licensed IP classified as filler — undermine original-artist positioning."
- The brand's enemy is generic, not expensive. Licensed IP is the definition of generic — it is the same design available from dozens of competitors.

**What to do with them on the new site:**
- Do not remove from catalog (Shreyas and Harley decision, not mine)
- Do not feature on homepage, collection pages, or marketing materials
- Place in a single holding category: "Pop Culture" — navigable for customers who find them via search or know what they are looking for, but not actively promoted
- Do not invest in new photography for licensed IP products
- Do not create bundles that pair licensed IP with original products — it dilutes the brand story
- If licensed IP represents meaningful current revenue, flag to Harley for a phased deprioritization conversation

**Specific products confirmed as licensed filler:** Darth Vader Lapel Pin, Rick & Morty Lapel Pin, Spiderman Lapel Pin, Coca-Cola Lapel Pin, BTS Keychain, Friends Frame Fridge Magnet, Cartoon Network Card Sticker, Rick & Morty Sticker Pack, and all other products where the primary design element is a third-party IP.

---

#### Filler 2: Generic Food / Drink / Animal Themes (No Attitudinal Hook)

**What they are:** Products themed around sushi, cake, coffee, beer, rainbows, generic animals — where the design is pleasant but has no point of view. Not all food or animal products are filler — a "Gin" earring has a personality. A "Rainbow Cake Keychain" does not.

**The test:** Does the product say something specific about the person who chooses it, or does it just say "I like [thing]"? "I like sushi" is not an opinion. "Idiot Repellent" is.

**What to do with them:** Retain in catalog for long-tail search. Do not feature in collections or marketing. Andy should tag these as lower-priority when building collection pages.

---

#### Filler 3: Sports Club / Brand Merchandise (Manchester United, Manchester City)

**What they are:** Football club-themed magnets confirmed in Maria's catalog audit. Likely similar products exist in other categories.

**Why filler:** Same issue as licensed IP — these are someone else's brand. No original design equity, no artist story, and clubs like Manchester United have official merchandise channels that TPL cannot compete with on credibility. These attract a buyer who is shopping for Manchester United merchandise, not for The Product Lab — they will never become loyal brand customers.

**What to do with them:** Retain in catalog for search traffic. Do not feature anywhere.

---

#### Filler 4: Custom Keychains (B2B / Bulk Product)

**What they are:** A single confirmed "custom design" product for bulk/B2B orders.

**Why filler for now:** Corporate gifting and bulk custom orders require a completely different sales motion than D2C. Heyward's positioning explicitly excludes corporate gifting buyers from the three named segments at current scale: "Not viable at current scale. Revisit at ₹50L+ annual revenue." This product can remain in the catalog for inbound B2B inquiries but should not be featured or marketed.

## Task 2.5 — Collection Structure & Bundle Logic

### Collection Architecture Principles

Eight to twelve collections at launch. Every collection must:
1. Serve one primary audience segment (Loud Whisper, Thoughtful Gifter, or Micro-Collector)
2. Have a name that is a brand statement, not a category label ("No Filter" not "Attitude Products")
3. Contain at least 8 products and no more than 40 (below 8 feels sparse; above 40 feels like a dump)
4. Have a one-sentence "reason this collection exists" that Andy can use as the collection header copy
5. Cross product types where design families exist (same design in keychain + magnet + earring = one collection entry, not three)

The navigation philosophy: **Collections are the primary discovery layer. Product type is the secondary filter.** A first-time visitor should browse by "What kind of person am I / who am I shopping for?" not by "What physical object do I want?"

### Bundle Strategy

**The problem bundles solve:** A customer buying a single ₹199 keychain generates negative contribution margin on a COD order (Patrick, Section 3.3). A customer buying a ₹499 bundle of three items generates positive contribution margin on COD and meaningful contribution on prepaid. Bundles are the primary mechanism to push AOV above ₹499.

**Bundle design principles:**
- Every bundle must have a name and a reason to exist — not "Bundle A" or "Product Pack 1"
- Every bundle must have a gifting occasion story — the most likely bundle buyer is The Thoughtful Gifter
- Every bundle must price at ₹499, ₹699, or ₹999 — the three psychological anchor points above the free shipping threshold
- Every bundle should include at least one card sticker — it is the unique product and should be introduced to every customer

### Drop / Limited Edition Framework

**Purpose:** Create urgency, repeat visits, and Instagram content on a cadence. Serve the Micro-Collector. Generate social proof through sold-out signals.

**Drop cadence:** One drop per month. Announced on Instagram 72 hours before. Live for 72 hours or until sold out.

**Drop anatomy:**

| Element | Decision |
|---|---|
| Format | Lapel pins (primary drop format); card stickers can have seasonal drops |
| Artist | Named, with a one-line bio on the drop page — "Designed by [Name], based in [City]" |
| Quantity | 50-100 units per design. Small enough to sell out. Large enough to fulfill without production stress |
| Designs per drop | 3-7 designs per drop. Enough for the Micro-Collector to buy a set; few enough to feel scarce |
| Price | ₹299 per pin; ₹999 for the full drop set (5 designs) |
| Duration | 72 hours or sold out. No restocks. If it returns, it is a new edition — not the same drop |
| Sold-out behavior | Product stays visible with "Sold Out" badge. Archive page shows all past drops with sold-out status |
| Announcement | Instagram Reel (the drop design reveal) 72 hours before. WhatsApp broadcast to list 24 hours before. Email (if Klaviyo or Mailchimp is active) 2 hours before opening |

**Launch drop (Day 1):** Andy and Shreyas must confirm artist availability and production lead time before the relaunch date. The first drop does not have to be on launch day — but it should be within the first two weeks. The announcement that "the first drop is coming [date]" is part of the launch narrative.

**Drop naming convention:** Each drop is named for its theme, not numbered. Examples:
- "The Fine Art of Not Caring" (attitude/humor pins)
- "Bengaluru Blues" (city-identity pins, first local artist collaboration)
- "Summer Disconnect" (travel/nature pins, summer season)

This naming convention gives Nik and Casey (content) editorial hooks for every drop announcement.

## Commercial Rationale

### How this hierarchy drives AOV above ₹499

The current catalog structure makes single-item purchasing the default. There is no bundle, no cross-sell prompt, no collection architecture that suggests "get more than one." The new structure changes this at every touchpoint:

1. The free shipping threshold (₹499) is reinforced by bundle pricing at ₹499, ₹699, and ₹999. The customer who adds a ₹199 keychain to a cart is shown "Add ₹300 more for free shipping" with a direct link to recommended add-ons within the same collection.

2. Cross-format design families (Enjoy the ShitShow in keychain + magnet + earring) are merchandised together in the No Filter collection. A customer who loves the keychain sees the magnet and earring on the same page. This is a natural multi-item order.

3. Bundle pricing creates a psychological discount that also drives AOV: the Pin Set at ₹999 versus ₹1,495 individual is a 33% saving. The Birthday Box at ₹699 feels like a complete gift, not three separate products.

4. The card sticker multi-pack (₹399 for three stickers) introduces card stickers to customers who might only have bought one, while pushing the order toward the free shipping threshold.

### How this hierarchy serves the brand story

Every collection name and collection header copy in this document should be legible as a brand statement, not a category label. "No Filter" is not "Attitude Accessories." "Your Card Has a Personality" is not "Card Stickers." The collection names are the first layer of brand voice that a new visitor encounters. They must pass Heyward's test: "does this say what a competitor could say?" None of these names could be used by Dot Badges, Chumbak, or The Souled Store without sounding dishonest.

The deprioritization of licensed IP is also a brand statement. By moving Marvel, Star Wars, and Pokemon products out of the promotional hierarchy, the site communicates — visually, architecturally — that this brand makes its own work. A first-time visitor who browses three collections on this site and sees no licensed characters will correctly infer that the brand has original ideas. This inference is worth more than any tagline.

### What the Micro-Collector mechanic does over time

The Drop framework is the single most important mechanism for building repeat purchase behavior and an Instagram-native community. Each drop announcement is a content event. Each sold-out badge is social proof. Each returning collector is a repeat customer at ₹999 per drop. Over six months of monthly drops, a 50-person collector community generating one ₹999 purchase per month each adds ₹49,500/month to revenue — at a contribution margin above 60% on prepaid orders (Patrick, Section 3.1). The math is small but the compounding effect on brand equity is significant.
