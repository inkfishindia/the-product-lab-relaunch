## Copy Brief (Pre-Work Lock)

Before any word was written, these were confirmed from source documents:

- **One job for all copy:** Move a 19-26 year old urban Indian toward buying something that says who they are — and help their gifter friend do the same
- **Brand territory:** Small objects with big opinions (Heyward, brand-positioning.md)
- **Tagline:** Wear your opinion
- **Voice proof already in catalog:** "Bullshit Remover," "Idiot Repellent," "Enjoy the ShitShow," "Straight outta F#_ks to give" — the voice exists; this system names and extends it
- **Primary conversion path:** Instagram discovery → WhatsApp share with a friend → purchase (Weiss, customer-insight-report.md Section 4.5)
- **Words never to use:** quirky, unique (standalone), fun, curated, explore, premium, vibrant, everyday, special, amazing
- **Customer language to use:** "so me," "can't find this anywhere else," "care package," "tangible," "expressive" (Weiss, Section 3)
- **Price signal copy must support:** ₹249 for keychains and pins; language must make ₹249 feel like the right price, not a stretch

---

### Five Rules. No Exceptions.

**Rule 1: Have an opinion or stay quiet.**
Every sentence takes a position. "A keychain for everyday carry" is not copy — it is a product spec with a conjunction. "For the person who has already written off small talk as a waste of time" is copy. If a sentence could appear on a Chumbak product page or a Bewakoof category header without sounding wrong, cut it and start over.

**Rule 2: Talk like the smartest person in the group chat, not like a brand.**
The register is first-person-adjacent. Conspiratorial. Like a recommendation from a friend who found something before you did. Never corporate. Never "we are delighted to." Never "explore our curated range." Write it the way you would text it to one specific person who has good taste.

**Rule 3: The humor has an edge. Keep the edge.**
"Bullshit Remover" is the brand voice. "Smile More" is not. The audience wants honest, not sanitized. The line is not between funny and offensive — it is between honest and fake. Keep what is honest. Cut what is performing honesty while saying nothing. The humor never punches down at people. It punches at situations.

**Rule 4: Name the person. Not the product.**
Instead of "enamel keychain, double-sided, 40mm," write "for the one who reads the entire menu before deciding they want what they originally wanted." The product is not the hero. The person who picks it is. Specs go in, but they come second.

**Rule 5: Short enough to forward.**
If a product description cannot survive being pasted into a WhatsApp chat — if it loses all meaning without the product page context — it is too long or too brand-speak. Write for the forwarded message, not the full-page scroll. (Weiss, Section 6.4: WhatsApp is the conversion channel.)

---

### Voice by Context — Quick Reference

| Context | Register | Does this | Never does this |
|---|---|---|---|
| Homepage hero | Declarative, confident, short | States the brand's position in 6 words or fewer | Welcomes you to the store, invites you to explore |
| Collection page header | Opinionated, slightly conspiratorial | Frames who this collection is for before showing anything | Describes what the products are |
| Product page — headline | Pointed, identity-first | Names the person, not the object | Opens with material, size, or use case |
| Product page — description | Conversational, concrete, concise | Gets the reader to "yes, that's me" then gives facts | Tells a long story or uses adjectives that do no work |
| WhatsApp-shareable line | Short, forwarding-ready | Works as a standalone sentence without page context | Requires the reader to already know what the product is |
| Instagram caption | Punchy, meme-adjacent, screenshot-worthy | Earns the share before the link click | Starts with "We" or describes what the brand does |
| Instagram Story | Urgent, FOMO-adjacent, DM-casual | Creates a reason to act in the next 24 hours | Explains things |
| WhatsApp broadcast | Personal, direct, DM-from-a-friend | Gets to the point before the second line | Opens with the brand name, talks about the collection |
| Packaging insert | Warm, brief, private | Gratitude without gushing | Uses the same sharp edge as public copy — this is a private moment |
| Error / empty state | Wry, brief, never punishing | Acknowledges the moment without drama | Says "Oops!" or apologizes for existing |
| Out of stock / sold out | Matter-of-fact, slightly desirable | Makes the absence feel like evidence of quality | Apologizes or explains supply chain |

### Banned Words — Full List

These words are never used in any TPL copy. Not in product descriptions, not in captions, not in microcopy, not in emails, not in WhatsApp messages.

| Word | Why banned |
|---|---|
| quirky | Category wallpaper. Every competitor uses it. Zero differentiation. (Weiss) |
| unique (standalone) | Not a position — a wish. Use specifics instead. (Heyward) |
| fun | Surface-level. Says nothing about what kind of fun. (Heyward) |
| curated | Brand-speak. If we say it about our own products, it means nothing. |
| explore | Tells the customer what to do instead of giving them a reason to. |
| premium | Signals we are not sure the price is justified. |
| vibrant | Design-speak leaking into copy. Meaningless to the buyer. |
| everyday | Positions us as ordinary. We are not. |
| special | The vaguest positive adjective in English. |
| amazing | See "special." |
| Welcome to | Filler opening. Cut it and start with what matters. |
| Oops! | Do not narrate errors with cheerfulness. |
| Delight / delighted | Corporate-speak. We are not delighted. We are direct. |
| Journey | Brands do not have journeys. People do. |
| Passion | Every brand claims passion. Not a differentiator. |
| Innovative | Means nothing in accessories. |
| Elevate | Marketing filler. Banned on sight. |
| Thoughtfully designed | If we have to say it, we probably did not. |

## Part 2 — Product Page Copy

### PDP Template

Every product description follows this four-part structure. None of the parts is optional. All four should fit on a mobile screen before scrolling.

```
[IDENTITY HOOK — 1 sentence]
Who this is for. The specific person. An attitude, a behavior, a recognition moment.
Write this like you are pointing across the room: "Oh, this one's for you."

[WHY THIS / THE DETAIL — 1-2 sentences]
What makes this product the right physical object for that person.
Not what it is made of yet — what it says. What it does for the person's sense of self.
One specific thing that no generic brand would claim because it requires having a point of view.

[WHATSAPP LINE — 1 sentence, marked clearly for internal reference]
The sentence that works when forwarded. No brand name needed. No page context needed.
This is the line that travels. Keep it under 15 words.

[SPECS — last, always last]
Material. Size. Finish. Variants available.
Written as a list, not a paragraph. One line each.
Signal quality without apologizing for it.
```

**Structure in practice:** The hook opens. The "why this" deepens. The WhatsApp line travels. The specs close.

The spec section is not the product page — it is the confirmation after the customer has already said yes in their head.

## Part 7 — WhatsApp Message Templates

### Rules Before Writing

- WhatsApp copy is the most personal format in the system. It arrives in the same place as messages from close friends. It cannot be brand-speak.
- Every message must pass: "Would I forward this to someone?" If no, rewrite.
- No message starts with "Dear customer" or the brand name as a noun. Start with what the customer cares about.
- Use line breaks like you would in a real WhatsApp message — short paragraphs, not long blocks.
- Emoji: one maximum per message if used. Never decorative stacks.

## Part 9 — About Page

### Structure

The About page answers three questions in this order. No other structure is needed.

1. Who makes this?
2. Why does this brand exist?
3. What does this brand refuse to be?

The copy is honest, not polished. It reads like it was written by someone who actually knows the answer. Not a PR brief.

---

### About Page Full Draft

**Page headline:**
We make things that have something to say.

---

**Section 1: Who makes this?**

The Product Lab is a studio in Bengaluru.

Generic.

The humor here has edges. The designs are original. The artists are real people with names on their work.

## Part 14 — Copy Don'ts

*Every example below shows what the wrong version looks like, what is wrong with it, and what to write instead. This section is the enforcement mechanism for the voice rules.*

---

### Category: Product Descriptions

**Wrong:**
"Explore our curated collection of premium keychains, each uniquely designed to complement your vibrant personality."

**Why it's wrong:**
Uses four banned words (explore, curated, premium, vibrant) and one banned standalone use of "unique." The sentence describes the brand's curation, not the customer's situation. It could appear on any accessories site without changing a word.

**Right:**
"For the one who is done carrying generic on their keys. The No Filter keychain says the thing your face already says. Zinc alloy. 40mm. Double-sided enamel."

---

**Wrong:**
"Our card stickers are fun and functional! Perfect for adding a bit of personality to your everyday cards."

**Why it's wrong:**
Exclamation point on a product description. "Fun" is banned. "A bit of personality" undersells the only product in India of its kind. "Everyday cards" is generic and uses a banned construction.

**Right:**
"Your bank card hands to strangers every day. It is currently saying nothing. This card sticker changes that in five minutes. Premium vinyl. Waterproof. Residue-free removal."

---

### Category: Collection Headers

**Wrong:**
"Explore our amazing collection of attitude accessories. Find your perfect match!"

**Why it's wrong:**
"Explore" is banned. "Amazing" is banned. "Find your perfect match" is a dating app line, not an accessories collection header. The exclamation point is unearned.

**Right:**
"For when you're done pretending."

---

**Wrong:**
"The best gifts for your loved ones — thoughtfully curated and beautifully packaged."

**Why it's wrong:**
"Curated" is banned. "Loved ones" is a greeting card phrase, not customer language. "Beautifully packaged" tells them what to expect instead of making them want it.

**Right:**
"Gifts for people with opinions. Which is everyone you actually like."

---

### Category: Instagram Captions

**Wrong:**
"We are so excited to introduce our new No Filter collection! Packed with fun and unique pieces, this curated lineup is perfect for expressing your vibrant self. Shop now — link in bio!"

**Why it's wrong:**
Starts with "We." Uses "excited," "fun," "unique," "curated," "vibrant" — five words from the banned list. "Shop now" is weak. "Link in bio" is fine but comes after too much noise to matter.

**Right:**
"New in No Filter. For when polite has run its course. Link in bio."

---

**Wrong:**
"Did you know that card stickers are the perfect way to add some personality to your bank cards? Our premium vinyl stickers are waterproof and easy to remove!"

**Why it's wrong:**
"Did you know" is a listicle opener. "Perfect way" is vague. "Some personality" undersells. "Premium vinyl" and the specs belong on the product page, not the Instagram caption.

**Right:**
"Your debit card has been saying nothing for years. ₹149. Takes five minutes. No other brand in India makes these."

---

### Category: WhatsApp Messages

**Wrong:**
"Dear Valued Customer, Thank you for your recent purchase from The Product Lab. We are delighted to inform you that your order has been confirmed and will be dispatched shortly. We appreciate your support and hope you enjoy your purchase!"

**Why it's wrong:**
"Dear Valued Customer" is a corporate form letter in a personal messaging app. "Delighted" is banned. "Dispatched shortly" is vague. "We appreciate your support" is filler. This message could have been sent by any brand in any category with no changes.

**Right:**
"Your order is confirmed. [Product name] — ₹[amount]. We'll send a shipping update here when it dispatches. In the meantime: show someone what you got."

---

**Wrong:**
"Hey! Check out our amazing new drop — limited edition pins designed with passion and care by talented Indian artists! Shop now before they sell out! 🔥🔥🔥"

**Why it's wrong:**
"Amazing" is banned. "Passion and care" is banned construction. "Talented" is filler. Three fire emoji. Multiple exclamation points. The urgency is manufactured, not real.

**Right:**
"[Drop name] drops [day], [time]. [X] pins by [Artist Name], Bengaluru. [X] made per design. 72 hours only. theproductlab.in/drop"

---

### Category: About Page

**Wrong:**
"At The Product Lab, we are passionate about creating unique, quirky accessories that bring joy and vibrancy to everyday life. Our curated collection of premium products is lovingly crafted to help you express your authentic self on your unique journey."

**Why it's wrong:**
Every banned word in one paragraph: passionate, unique, quirky, vibrant, everyday, curated, premium. "Lovingly crafted" is craft beer copy. "Authentic self on your unique journey" is the worst sentence we could possibly publish.

**Right:**
See Part 9 — About Page. The question "why does this brand exist" should be answered with a fact, not an aspiration.

## Part 15 — Naming Conventions

*The system that produced "Bullshit Remover" should be repeatable. This section documents that system so Dan can name new products, collections, and drops without a copywriter.*

---

### The Framework: What Kind of Name Does This Product Need?

Every product, collection, or drop name starts with one question: **what does this say about the person who picks it?**

Names fall into three categories. Use the right category for the right product.

---

**Category 1: The Attitude Name**

*Used for:* No Filter collection products, attitude keychains, anything in the brand's humor franchise.

*How it works:* Name the function or attitude of the person who owns this, stated directly and honestly. Not a description of the object. A description of the owner's internal state.

| Object description | Attitude name |
|---|---|
| Keychain for someone frustrated by stupidity | Idiot Repellent |
| Keychain for someone exhausted by BS | Bullshit Remover |
| Object for someone who has accepted chaos | Enjoy the ShitShow |
| Keychain for someone who has no patience left | Straight outta F#_ks to give |

*The test:* Would the owner laugh when they saw the name and immediately think "that's me"? If yes, it works. If they would need it explained, it does not.

*What to avoid:*
- Adjective names ("Sassy," "Bold," "Fierce") — describe the brand, not the owner's specific situation
- Generic humor ("LOL," "No Chill" without context) — funny to no one in particular
- Names that require cultural translation — if a non-Indian Gen Z would not get it, reconsider

---

**Category 2: The Identity Name**

*Used for:* Theme collections, subculture products, the "who you are" product tier.

*How it works:* Name the product after what the owner is into, stated with confidence — not as a category label but as a declaration. The name does the work of saying "this was made for you specifically."

| Generic label | Identity name |
|---|---|
| Sneaker-themed products | Sneakerhead Edit |
| K-Pop themed products | K-Pop Drop |
| Gaming-themed products | The 1UP Set |
| Books-themed products | Overdue |

*The test:* Would someone in that subculture feel like this was made specifically for them — not for everyone who vaguely "likes" the category? If yes, it works.

---

**Category 3: The Drop Name**

*Used for:* Limited-edition drops, monthly releases, artist series.

*How it works:* Name the drop for its cultural or emotional theme — not for its product type or artist name. The drop name is the story. It is the hook for the Instagram announcement. It should be a phrase a collector would remember and reference ("I got the Bengaluru Blues set").

*Drop name formula:* [Attitude or feeling + Cultural reference or setting]

| Drop theme | Drop name |
|---|---|
| Chaos acceptance | The Fine Art of Not Caring |
| Bengaluru identity | Bengaluru Blues |
| Summer + disconnecting | Summer Disconnect |
| Digital fatigue | Out of Wifi |
| Overcommitment | I'll Sleep When I'm Dead |

*The test:* Does the name work as an Instagram caption on its own? Can you post just the drop name and have people ask "what is this?" If yes, it works.

*What to avoid:*
- Numbered drops ("Drop #4") — no story, no reason to care
- Seasonal generic names ("Summer Collection," "Festive Edit") — competitor copy
- Artist-name-first drops — the artist attribution goes on the product page; the drop name is the narrative

---

### Naming Rules Summary

1. Names are not adjectives. A product named "Bold" means nothing. A product named "Idiot Repellent" means something specific.
2. Names should work without seeing the product. "Bullshit Remover" tells you what you need to know before you see the keychain.
3. Names should pass the "would the wrong person buy this?" test. If the name is specific enough, the wrong customer will self-select out. That is the point.
4. Collection names are positioning statements, not category labels. "No Filter" is not "Attitude Accessories."
5. Drop names are stories, not product descriptions. "Bengaluru Blues" is not "Bengaluru-themed pins."

---

### Quick Reference: Name vs. Description

| This is a description — do not use as the primary name | This is a name |
|---|---|
| Attitude Keychain Collection | No Filter |
| Card Sticker Set | Your Card Has a Personality |
| Limited Edition Pins | The Fine Art of Not Caring |
| K-Pop Products | K-Pop Drop |
| Birthday Gift Bundle | The Birthday Box |
| Best-Selling Items | Best Sellers (fine — operational, not creative) |
| Starter Pack | The Opinion Starter Pack |
