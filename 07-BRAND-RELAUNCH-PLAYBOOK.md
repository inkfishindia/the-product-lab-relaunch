# The Product Lab Brand Relaunch Playbook

## 1. Objective

This playbook turns the operating system into a The Product Lab-specific execution plan. It is based on the current working hypothesis that the brand’s upside lies in becoming a sharper, more collectible, more culturally legible lifestyle brand rather than remaining a broad merchandise store with weak hierarchy.

## 2. Current-State Diagnosis

The Product Lab appears to have product-level potential but weak brand-level compression. The products suggest expressiveness, gifting, and youth-oriented design energy, but the storefront and messaging likely do not yet create a strong enough answer to the customer question, “Why this brand?”

The most probable issues are:

- broad, generic value propositions
- unclear hero categories
- insufficient product storytelling
- limited visual distinctiveness
- weak brand-first orientation on the site
- no obvious relaunch narrative that would reintroduce the brand with force

## 3. Strategic Direction

The relaunch should position The Product Lab as an expressive everyday-objects brand for customers who want their things to feel more personal, graphic, giftable, and culturally alive. This does not mean the brand should become vague “lifestyle energy.” It means products should be merchandised as artifacts of taste and identity, not just low-ticket items.

The brand must decide whether its strongest edge comes from:

- artist-led expression
- collectible desk and lifestyle objects
- drop-driven limited collections
- gifting and self-expression
- pop-cultural irreverence

The role of strategy is to choose the dominant thesis, not to keep all of them equally active.

## 4. Brand Pillars

These are recommended starting pillars that should be validated during strategy work:

### Expressive Utility

Products should live in daily life but carry more attitude and identity than generic functional objects.

### Collectible Energy

Small-format items should feel collectible, giftable, and easy to build attachment to.

### Culture-Aware Design

The brand should feel connected to young visual culture without looking derivative or trend-dependent.

### Accessible Desire

Products should feel desirable and presentable without requiring luxury pricing.

## 5. Product and Merchandising Hypothesis

The brand should probably not lead with every category equally. Instead, it should structure the catalog into:

- hero categories that define the brand
- support categories that increase basket size
- filler categories that are paused, reduced, or hidden from primary storytelling

The team should identify:

- best visual categories
- best margin categories
- best repeat-purchase or gifting categories
- best social-content categories

Those categories should drive homepage, navigation, campaign, and launch sequencing.

## 6. Website Principles

The rebuilt site should follow these principles:

### Brand-first homepage

The homepage should explain the world, the signature collections, and the most desirable products. It should not function mainly as a generic catalog feed.

### Editorial collection pages

Collection pages should feel like curated destinations with story, not just filters.

### Strong product pages

Every PDP should explain why the product exists, what makes it desirable, how it looks in context, and what else should be bought with it.

### Mobile-first clarity

The experience must be fast and legible on mobile because discovery and impulse are likely mobile-heavy.

### Launch and drop readiness

The system must support campaigns, gifting moments, limited drops, and featured edits without heavy rework.

## 7. Relaunch Narrative

The relaunch should not be framed as “new website live.” It should be framed as a sharper version of The Product Lab arriving with a point of view.

Potential narrative frames:

- The Product Lab, re-engineered for self-expression
- Everyday objects with more attitude
- Small things, stronger personality
- The new Product Lab drop

Final messaging should be decided only after brand strategy is approved.

## 8. Recommended Launch Sequence

### Stage 1: Quiet Research and Build

- complete audit
- define audience and hero collections
- design and build new storefront

### Stage 2: Prelaunch Signal Building

- tease the evolution visually
- build waitlist or early access capture
- seed products to a small creator set
- begin social world-building around collections

### Stage 3: Launch Week

- announce the brand reset
- release hero collection or curated edit
- drive traffic to strong landing pages
- push email, social, creator, and paid in coordinated waves

### Stage 4: Post-launch Learning

- refine underperforming page sections
- adjust bundles and merchandising
- identify which launch hooks earned the best customers

## 9. Immediate Workstreams

1. Audit the current website page by page.
2. Pull category, product, and conversion baselines.
3. Identify hero category candidates.
4. Build a competitor and inspiration board across India and global youth/lifestyle brands.
5. Write the positioning options before starting final visual design.
6. Build homepage and PDP wireframes around the chosen product hierarchy.

## 10. What Success Looks Like

Customers should be able to understand The Product Lab quickly, remember it clearly, and want to browse beyond a single SKU. The relaunch is successful when the brand feels more ownable, the site converts more confidently, hero products become obvious, and the launch creates enough momentum to reset perception in the market.
