# The Product Lab Relaunch Master Brief

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-03-14  
**Initiative Owner:** Program Director / CEO Agent  
**Project Type:** Brand relaunch + ecommerce rebuild + growth reset

## 1. Executive Intent

The Product Lab has underlying potential but is not currently presenting itself as a sharp, differentiated, high-conviction lifestyle brand. The likely problem is not simply design quality. The deeper issue is that the brand signal, product hierarchy, storytelling, and commercial architecture are not reinforcing one another strongly enough. A full relaunch is justified only if the business uses the rebuild to clarify what it stands for, what it sells best, who it is for, and how the site converts first-time visitors into repeat buyers.

This program exists to turn The Product Lab from a broad merchandise storefront into a recognizable, ownable brand with stronger product merchandising, tighter visual identity, better conversion fundamentals, and a launch campaign that creates attention instead of merely publishing a new website.

## 2. Core Business Opportunity

The brand appears to sit in a useful consumer space: expressive, artist-led, youth-facing lifestyle products that can live across gifting, self-expression, collectibles, desk objects, stickers, accessories, and culture-adjacent merchandise. That is a strong territory, but only if it is made legible.

The opportunity is to build a brand that occupies a specific emotional job:

- helping customers signal taste and identity in everyday objects
- making small-format products feel collectible and giftable
- creating repeat engagement through drops, collaborations, and themed collections
- converting a broad SKU mix into a coherent branded world

If executed correctly, the upside is not only a better website. The upside is improved conversion rate, stronger average order value, clearer hero categories, more useful paid traffic economics, and a brand that customers can describe to one another in a sentence.

## 3. Working Hypothesis

The current business is likely underperforming because of a combination of five issues:

1. The positioning is too broad to be memorable.
2. The catalog lacks enough visible hierarchy between hero, support, and filler products.
3. The website behaves like a product repository instead of a brand experience.
4. The visual system does not create enough desirability or signature recall.
5. The launch and retention engine are weaker than the brand’s inherent potential.

This initiative assumes that solving those five issues together will produce materially better outcomes than optimizing them independently.

## 4. Initiative Goals

The relaunch should achieve the following goals:

- define a precise brand position that is specific, repeatable, and commercially useful
- identify and elevate the most ownable product categories and collections
- rebuild the ecommerce experience around discovery, desire, and conversion
- produce a visual and verbal identity that is more distinctive than the current expression
- create a relaunch campaign with enough energy to reset customer perception
- install the growth and measurement systems needed for post-launch iteration

## 5. Non-Goals

To keep this program disciplined, the following are out of scope unless explicitly approved:

- building a giant, multi-quarter replatform before clarifying the offer
- adding large custom technical systems that do not directly improve merchandising or conversion
- expanding into many new product categories before hero categories are validated
- launching a broad paid acquisition push without site, offer, and product-story alignment
- writing a purely aesthetic brand book disconnected from commerce mechanics

## 6. Success Metrics

The exact baselines should be established during audit. Until then, the team should work toward directional targets:

- homepage bounce rate reduction
- collection page engagement increase
- product page conversion lift
- cart-to-checkout progression improvement
- overall conversion rate increase
- average order value increase through bundles and merchandising
- email or waitlist growth ahead of launch
- percentage of revenue from hero categories
- repeat purchase rate over the first 90-180 days after relaunch
- improvement in branded search, direct traffic, or community engagement signals

## 7. Constraints and Operating Assumptions

- The relaunch must be grounded in real consumer and catalog insight, not taste alone.
- The team should prefer a focused assortment and clear collection strategy over catalog sprawl.
- The website must be mobile-first and operationally realistic.
- The content system should support launches, drops, gifting moments, and editorial storytelling.
- Technical decisions should preserve maintainability and speed.
- The visual language should feel intentional and confident, not generic DTC minimalism.

## 8. Strategic Questions the Team Must Answer

Before the design system or build starts, the team must answer the following:

1. Who exactly is the primary customer, and what do they value beyond “cute” or “unique” products?
2. Which product categories deserve hero treatment, and which should be reduced, grouped, or deprioritized?
3. What does The Product Lab stand for that adjacent brands do not?
4. How should the site balance store utility with world-building and editorial discovery?
5. What is the relaunch narrative that makes customers care that the brand is back or evolving?
6. What repeatable growth loops can support the business after the launch burst ends?

## 9. Program Structure

The work will run in six phases:

1. Audit and Truth-Finding
2. Brand Strategy
3. Experience and Identity Design
4. Build and Merchandising Implementation
5. Relaunch Campaign and Go-Live
6. Optimization and Learning

Each phase has a decision gate. No phase should be treated as complete because artifacts exist. A phase completes only when the output is decision-ready and commercially useful.

## 10. Core Deliverables

The program should produce:

- audit report
- competitor and reference analysis
- audience segmentation and insight pack
- brand positioning statement
- messaging architecture
- visual direction and identity system
- sitemap and information architecture
- homepage, collection, product, and content page wireframes
- final storefront design
- production-ready site build
- content and copy system
- relaunch campaign assets and calendar
- measurement dashboard and testing roadmap

## 11. Governance Model

The Program Director is the final decision-maker. The Brand Strategist owns positioning. The Creative Director owns visual interpretation. The UX / Conversion Lead owns user journey quality. The Build Lead owns technical execution. The Growth Lead owns launch and acquisition mechanics. The Customer Insight Lead owns research truth. Shared ownership is not allowed where final judgment must be made; every major output should have a single accountable owner.

## 12. Key Risks

This initiative is vulnerable to several predictable failure modes:

- trying to solve brand confusion with visual polish alone
- keeping too many products or messages because internal stakeholders do not want to cut
- overbuilding before message-market clarity exists
- allowing the relaunch to become “website completion” instead of “brand reset”
- producing a launch campaign without enough narrative tension or freshness
- failing to instrument the business well enough to learn from the relaunch

## 13. Mitigation Principles

- cut aggressively where the offer is muddy
- test early with real people and real product pages
- keep hero products and hero stories visible throughout the build
- insist on explicit decision logs at each phase gate
- measure what changes, not just what ships
- treat launch as the midpoint, not the finish line

## 14. Immediate Next Steps

1. Complete a site and catalog audit.
2. Gather baseline commercial metrics and customer evidence.
3. Identify direct and adjacent brand references.
4. Define primary and secondary audience segments.
5. Run positioning and assortment decisions before final UI work starts.
